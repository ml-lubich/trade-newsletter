Two years ago, if you told me I'd be spending my mornings on HVAC job sites and my evenings writing a newsletter about plumbing software, I would have thought you were confused. I was deep in the typical Bay Area tech orbit — building ML pipelines, optimizing recommendation systems, going to AI meetups where everyone was building chatbots for other tech companies.

Then I had a conversation that changed everything.

## The Call That Started It All

My uncle runs a mid-size electrical contracting business in Sacramento. Fifteen employees, $2.5M annual revenue, 30 years in business. During a family dinner, he mentioned he was paying a guy $25/hour to manually enter data from paper job forms into QuickBooks.

"Wait," I said. "How many forms per week?"

"About 80-100."

I nearly dropped my fork. This was a straightforward OCR + data extraction problem. The kind of thing I could prototype in a weekend with existing AI APIs. But to my uncle, it was just "how things work."

I built him a simple tool that weekend. Photo of the form → extracted data → pushed to QuickBooks. Rough edges everywhere, but it worked. His data entry guy got redeployed to actually useful work.

**That tool saved his business $52,000 a year.**

## Going Down the Rabbit Hole

I started asking my uncle's friends — other trade business owners — what else was painful. The answers came flooding in:

- "I spend every Sunday writing estimates instead of being with my family"
- "I lost a $40K contract because my proposal looked unprofessional"
- "I have no idea which marketing channels are actually bringing in jobs"
- "My best tech is retiring next year and his knowledge is going to walk out the door with him"

These weren't edge cases. These were universal problems shared by virtually every small trade business in America. And the software options available to them were either enterprise-grade and expensive (ServiceTitan at $500+/month) or consumer-grade and underpowered (generic invoicing apps).

## The Gap in the Market is a Canyon

I started researching the space more seriously. What I found shocked me:

- **73% of trade businesses** with under 20 employees use fewer than 3 software tools
- The average trade business owner spends **15+ hours per week** on administrative tasks
- **60% of customer complaints** in trades relate to communication (not workmanship)
- Less than **5% of trade businesses** use any form of AI or automation

For context, the restaurant industry — which has comparable business sizes and tech-skeptical owners — is at 35% AI/automation adoption. Trades are a decade behind.

## Why Trades Are Different (And Why That's an Advantage)

Building for trade businesses requires a fundamentally different approach than building for tech companies or even other SMBs. Here's what I've learned:

### Trust is Everything
Trade business owners have been burned by software salespeople. They've bought tools that promised the world and delivered nothing. They need to see your product work on THEIR data, for THEIR business, before they'll commit.

### The Buying Process is Personal
There's no procurement department. The decision-maker is the owner, and they're making the call between your software and a new truck payment. Your product needs to have obvious, immediate ROI.

### Mobile Isn't a Feature — It's the Product
These businesses run from trucks. If your app requires sitting at a desktop, you've already lost. Everything needs to work on a phone, often with one hand, often in bad lighting.

### Simplicity Wins Over Features
The worst thing you can do is build a feature-rich dashboard that takes 3 hours to set up. Trade business owners want tools that work in 5 minutes with zero training. If the newest tech on the job site can't figure it out, it's too complicated.

## What I'm Building Now

I can't share all the details yet (soon), but here's the broad direction:

I'm building AI-powered tools that take the administrative burden off small trade businesses. Think of it as a "second brain" for contractors — it handles the estimates, the follow-ups, the scheduling optimization, the review management, and the business intelligence that these owners currently do manually or not at all.

The key insight is that most of these tasks share a common structure: **take unstructured information (photos, conversations, job notes) and turn it into structured, actionable outputs (estimates, invoices, reports, marketing content).**

That's exactly what modern AI is good at.
