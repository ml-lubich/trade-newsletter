There's a pattern in tech that repeats every decade or so. Some massive, obvious market sits right under everyone's noses, and the entire venture capital ecosystem ignores it because it doesn't look like a Silicon Valley problem.

In 2010, it was restaurants. Everyone said "restaurants are low-margin, low-tech, they'll never adopt software." Then Toast went public at a $20B valuation.

In 2015, it was construction. "Too fragmented, too resistant to change." Procore is now worth $10B+.

**In 2026, it's local trade services. And almost nobody is building for them.**

## The Size of the Opportunity

Let's do the math that VCs aren't doing:

- **1.2 million** licensed HVAC, plumbing, and electrical businesses in the US
- Average revenue per business: **$850K-$1.2M/year**
- Current software spend per business: **$2,800/year**
- Willingness to spend on tools that demonstrably increase revenue: **$8,000-$15,000/year**

That gap — between $2,800 and $12,000 — is the opportunity. Multiply it by 1.2 million businesses and you get a **$11-15B annual recurring revenue opportunity** just in the core trades. Add in roofing, landscaping, pest control, garage doors, and other home services, and you're looking at **$40-50B in addressable market**.

## Why Nobody is Building Here

The reasons VCs ignore this market are the same reasons it's so attractive:

### 1. The Customers Don't Look Like Tech Customers
Trade business owners don't hang out on Twitter/X. They don't read TechCrunch. They don't attend SaaStr. They're on job sites at 6 AM and doing invoices at 8 PM. Finding them requires going where they are — trade shows, supply houses, Facebook groups, local business associations.

### 2. The Sales Motion is "Wrong"
Enterprise SaaS has trained a generation of founders to think in terms of self-serve PLG or top-down enterprise sales. Trades require a middle path: high-touch onboarding with boots-on-the-ground sales. That's expensive and doesn't look good in a pitch deck.

### 3. The Tech is "Boring"
Nobody gets featured in Hacker News for building a better dispatching tool for plumbers. But the plumber who cuts their scheduling overhead by 30% doesn't care about your Hacker News karma — they care about going home earlier.

## Where AI Changes Everything

Here's where it gets interesting. The trades have historically been underserved by software because the problems are complex and messy:

- **Estimating** requires domain expertise that's hard to encode
- **Scheduling** involves dynamic constraints (weather, permits, parts availability)
- **Customer communication** needs to be personalized but owners don't have time
- **Marketing** requires local expertise that national agencies don't have

AI is the unlock that makes all of these tractable. An AI that's been trained on 100,000 HVAC estimates can price a job in seconds. An LLM that understands a contractor's voice can write follow-up emails that sound human. A vision model can look at a photo of a roof and generate a repair scope.

## The Playbook for Building in This Space

If you're a founder thinking about this market, here's what I'd tell you:

1. **Embed yourself in the industry first.** Spend 3 months working with or shadowing trade businesses. The nuances are everything.
2. **Start with one vertical.** HVAC, plumbing, electrical — pick one and go deep. Horizontal plays fail because the workflows are too different.
3. **Solve a revenue problem, not a cost problem.** Trades will pay for tools that bring in more jobs. They're skeptical of tools that just "save time."
4. **Build for mobile.** These are truck-based businesses. If it doesn't work on an iPhone in a parking lot, it doesn't work.
5. **Price based on value, not seats.** A 5-person plumbing company and a 50-person company have very different needs but often the same number of people making decisions.

## The Window is Open — But Closing

ServiceTitan's IPO in late 2025 put a spotlight on this space. The big players are expanding their AI capabilities. But ServiceTitan is enterprise-focused — they're going after the $5M+ revenue companies. The long tail of 1-15 employee trade businesses is still wide open.

If I were starting a company today, this is exactly where I'd build.
